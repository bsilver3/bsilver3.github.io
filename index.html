The Magical Time-Bending Phenomenon of Chronostasis

Recall that incredibly focused moment, during the final moments of a project, test, or deadline.

The analog clock just out of your vision silently ticks away, reminding you to focus, although you don’t know how much time remains. You are “locked in”. Nothing can interrupt your intense focus.

However, without the audible rhythm of the clock ticking, you quickly shift your attention to the clock to check how much time is left.

Wait- what!? It looked like the second hand stopped for a bit!?

That brief moment when time seems to stretch, and you're left wondering if you're trapped in a simulation where someone accidentally hit a pause button – that's chronostasis at play.
It's like a magic trick your brain pulls when you're laser-focused on something, and then, surprise, a new thing pops up.

If this mysterious visual phenomenon fascinates you, join me on this journey as we unravel the secrets behind this captivating illusion and discover the hidden rhythms of time in the everyday.

Let's start with the whole reason that this effect takes place in the first place: your eyes.

Your eyes and your brain have a unique connection with each other. Your eyes directly feed your brain data, and your brain processes that data, then identifies patterns found in that data.

Your eyes also have a very special movement that they do, called a saccadic eye movement.

In this movement, both eyes move at the same time from one point of fixation towards another. This movement is incredibly fast, peaking at angular velocities up to 700 degrees per second.

Your eyes perform this movement multiple times per second, and this is what allows your brain to gather the most information that it can.
This is why if you look at someone's eyes when they are watching a movie, their eyes will look like they are "jumping around".

Because I want to see how the speed of these movements affects chronostasis, let's take a deeper look into these movements.

This means that the further away an object is, the faster the eyes will move to it.

Okay, so now we know that there's a relationship between these two random values.
Now how does this relate to chronostasis?

As the eyes move from point to point, they create a blur.

This blur is directly related to the amplitude of the eye movement.

The further an eye moves, the longer there will be a blur.
This blur will also get stronger as the eye movement gets faster, meaning the eye will take in less information.

From the data that we looked at, we know that there is a positive association between the amplitude and velocity of a saccadic eye movement.
Because velocity will get larger as amplitude increases (and vice versa), this means that this blur will also get stronger and longer if the amplitude increases.
So, in simple terms, a bigger eye movement equals a bigger blur.

As humans, we don’t actually see this blur because of how our brain works. The brain doesn’t actually get any visual information from this blur.

Because the brain wants a constant flow of visual information, it will try to fill in this gap where the blur is with what it thinks should go there.

Since the brain can’t properly interpret data like this, it will fill in this gap of data with what it can see fully after the eye movement. This means that the object will appear “frozen in time”, because the same image of the object will appear for longer than it is in reality.

So let’s recap- what is chronostasis?

Chronostasis is a common experience where things seem to freeze in time for a moment.
It occurs when you shift your eyes from one thing to another, and your brain fills in details from the time your eyes are moving.

This "freezing" effect is more noticeable when you look at something far away, since your eyes move longer distances, requiring your brain to fill in more information.

We know that this effect seems random when it happens, and it takes you off guard when it does occur. However, there's an easy way to replicate this effect.
To try it yourself, start by concentrating on one thing, then quickly shift your focus to another thing that is out of your vision, like a clock on the wall. If time seems to "freeze" on the second hand for a short period of time, then you have successfully experienced a form of chronostasis.
But remember, the real surprise happens when it catches you off guard, since your brain will be ready for your staged attempts.
